FIRE AND ALLIED PERILS

Common Fire and Lightning policy may appear to cover all "Fire" and "Lightning" losses. However, fineprints may contain exclusions which are usually included so as to mitigate insurance company's losses.

The following are usually excluded unless specifically covered:

  • Electrical Short Circuiting, Arcing, and the like, even if it is due to lightning unless fire ensues.
  • Spontaneous combustion
  • Explosion
  • Forest or Bush fires
  • Damaged Money, Securities, Plans & Drawings, Artworks

Most of the time, property owners does not conduct thorough inspection of their property unless an incident occurs. After an earthquake, it is common to suddenly be concerned about hairline cracks found within their property and engage contractors to check on these cracks. It is important to conduct regular inspection of the property and maintenance works to minimize losses.

Although you could purchase an insurance policy that covers earthquake loss, it will always be impossible to claim 100% of loss from the insurance provider as there are government mandated policy deductibles (or participation) set to ensure that insurance companies could continue their businesses even after large scale disasters.

Fineprints of common Property Insurance that extends to cover Typhoon Loss usually carries the following exclusions:

  • Damage to contents of the building due to rain unless the building itself sustained damage first
  • Flooding unless a Flood cover is included in the policy
  • Water damage due to backing of sewer, drains and pipes
  • Water damage due to Doors, windows left open and/or any other openings of the building.

Although you could purchase an insurance policy that covers typhoon loss, it will always be impossible to claim 100% of loss from the insurance provider as there are government mandated policy deductibles (or participation) set to ensure that insurance companies could continue their businesses even after large scale disasters.

Flood cover usually comes in conjunction with Typhoon cover. Similar to typhoon, it will always be impossible to claim 100% of loss from the insurance provider as there are government mandated policy deductibles (or participation) set to ensure that insurance companies could continue their businesses even after large scale disasters.

Flood is actually defined under common policy extensions. Thus, any other event which we may call "flood" may not be covered by these policies. The following are defined as flood

  • Flooding following a Typhoon as categorized by the weather bureau
  • Extra ordinary high tide inundating land not usually covered by water
  • Bursting or overflowing of rivers, reservoirs, canals and the like.
